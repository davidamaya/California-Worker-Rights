The headline numbers

Minimum wage in California

The statewide floor applies to every employer, regardless of size. Some industries and most large cities require more.

Statewide
$16.90/hr
Applies to all employers and adjusts each January 1 for inflation (Lab. Code §1182.12).
Fast food
$20.00/hr
For national chains with 60 or more locations, set by the Fast Food Council (AB 1228).
Health care
$18.63/hr
Varies by facility type, up to $24 (SB 525). Most tiers rise July 1, 2026, and the largest systems reach $25.
Exempt salary
$70,304/yr
The minimum salary to classify an employee as exempt, set at twice the state minimum wage for full-time work.
▲

You are always entitled to the highest minimum wage that applies to you. The federal rate is $7.25, but California's $16.90 takes precedence, and a higher city or industry rate takes precedence over that. For example, a retail worker in San Francisco is owed the city's $19.18 rather than the state's $16.90.

Worker protections

Your rights as a California worker

California law protects far more than your hourly rate. These are the protections workers ask about most.

Overtime pay

Time and a half for hours over 8 in a day or 40 in a week, and for the first 8 hours of a seventh consecutive workday. Double time beyond 12 hours in a day, or beyond 8 on that seventh day.

LC §510IWC Wage Orders

Meal periods

An unpaid 30-minute meal period before the end of the fifth hour when you work more than 5 hours, and a second before the tenth hour when you work more than 10. If a meal period is not provided, you are owed one additional hour of pay at your regular rate.

LC §512LC §226.7IWC Wage Orders

Rest periods

A paid 10-minute rest period for every 4 hours worked, or major fraction thereof. If a rest period is not provided, you are owed one additional hour of pay at your regular rate.

LC §226.7IWC Wage Orders

Paid sick leave

You accrue at least 1 hour of paid sick leave for every 30 hours worked, and may use at least 40 hours (5 days) per year. Employers may cap accrual but cannot deny the minimum.

LC §246SB 616

Your final paycheck

If you are discharged, all wages are due immediately. If you quit with at least 72 hours' notice, they are due on your last day; without notice, within 72 hours. Late final pay can trigger a waiting-time penalty of up to 30 days' wages.

LC §201LC §202LC §203

If your rights are violated

You can file a wage claim with the California Labor Commissioner's Office (DLSE) at no cost and without an attorney. It is unlawful for an employer to fire or retaliate against you for asserting these rights.

DLSELC §98.6LC §1102.5

These summaries describe California wage and hour rules in general terms; the rules can turn on specific facts, exemptions, and the applicable wage order. For guidance on your situation, contact the California Labor Commissioner's Office or a qualified employment attorney.

Local rates change frequently, and many cities adjust again on July 1. Always confirm the current figure for your worksite with the city or county directly.
